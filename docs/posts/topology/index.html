Understanding the Fundamental Group

Topology
Algebraic Topology
Author

Tiago Verissimo

Published

June 11, 2026

In algebraic topology, one of the most basic invariants used to study topological spaces is the fundamental group. It captures information about the 1-dimensional “holes” in a space by studying the behaviour of loops.

Intuition: Loops on a Surface

Imagine placing a loop of string on a surface: * On a sphere (like a football), any loop you draw can be shrunk down continuously to a single point. * On a torus (the surface of a donut), a loop that wraps around the tube or around the central hole cannot be shrunk to a point without cutting the surface or the string.

This distinction is formalized by the fundamental group, denoted \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\).

Formal Definition

Let \(X\) be a topological space, and let \(x_0 \in X\) be a basepoint. A loop based at \(x_0\) is a continuous map \(f: [0, 1] \to X\) such that \(f(0) = f(1) = x_0\).

Two loops \(f\) and \(g\) are homotopic (written \(f \simeq g\)) if there exists a continuous map (a homotopy) \(H: [0, 1] \times [0, 1] \to X\) such that: * \(H(s, 0) = f(s)\) for all \(s \in [0, 1]\) * \(H(s, 1) = g(s)\) for all \(s \in [0, 1]\) * \(H(0, t) = H(1, t) = x_0\) for all \(t \in [0, 1]\)

The elements of the fundamental group \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) are the homotopy classes of loops, denoted \([f]\).

The group operation is concatenation of loops (running one loop after the other at double speed):

\[(f * g)(s) = \begin{cases} f(2s) & 0 \le s \le \frac{1}{2} \\ g(2s - 1) & \frac{1}{2} \le s \le 1 \end{cases}\]

ImportantAlgebraic Invariants

If two spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) are homeomorphic, their fundamental groups are isomorphic: \[\pi_1(X, x_0) \cong \pi_1(Y, y_0)\] For instance, the fundamental group of a circle \(S^1\) is isomorphic to the group of integers \(\mathbb{Z}\), while the fundamental group of a sphere \(S^2\) is trivial (the group containing only the identity).
